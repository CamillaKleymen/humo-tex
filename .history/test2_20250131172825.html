Abu Dhabi Investment Authority (ADIA) — $773 billion

ADIA has never published the value of its assets, but experts estimate them at between $650 billion and $875 billion. It was founded in 1976. Due to its size, the fund has significant influence on international finance.

It receives 70% of the budget surplus each year, as well as dividends from the state oil monopoly ADNOC. 

The bulk of its funds are invested in shares of developed market companies and US government bonds. The fund manages the emirate's excess income from oil exports, which is estimated at almost $1 trillion.

China Investment Corporation — $746.7 billion

The fund was created in 2007 to manage part of China's gold and foreign exchange reserves (≈$200 billion) with a higher return than a conservative strategy of investing in US government bonds provides. 

Over 30% of CIC's portfolio is in stocks, primarily in financial and IT companies. About 40% of all assets are placed abroad, largely with a stake in the recovery of the US and European economies. 

The company's main assets are invested on behalf of the government in the domestic market, mostly in large corporations.

A significant part of the portfolio has long been invested in energy companies and projects due to their strategic importance. However, after the crisis, such investments stopped yielding the expected returns.

Recently, the fund's investment spectrum has shifted towards investing in agriculture, and vertically, throughout the entire industry, from planting and irrigation to production of final products and livestock breeding. Investment objects are located all over the world, without being tied to individual countries.

Kuwait Investment Authority — $548 billion

The volume of the Kuwait Investment Authority (KIA) fund is $548 billion. 

It has existed since 1953 and is considered the oldest sovereign fund in the world.  Includes the Reserve Fund, which acts as a treasury and owner of all state property, and the Future Generations Fund, which annually receives 10% of all state revenues. 

Among its major assets are stakes in Daimler AG, BP, and Bank of America Merrill Lynch. 

China SAFE Investment Company — $547 billion

It includes the Reserve Fund, which acts as a treasury and the owner of all state property, and the Future Generations Fund, which annually receives 10% of all state revenues. 

The company's board of directors includes official representatives of the administration. The fund is active in the British stock market, its portfolio includes shares of Royal Dutch Shell, Rio Tinto, Barclays, BHP Billiton. First of all, the fund is China's reserve currency fund. 

The main objectives of the fund are to generate additional income on the fund's assets, expand the diversification of investments, and reduce the dependence of the Chinese economy on dollar fluctuations. SAFE can invest in a wide range of instruments: shares of international and local companies, bonds and other debt instruments.

Hong Kong Monetary Authority Exchange Fund — $400.2 billion

The Hong Kong Monetary Authority Exchange Fund is $400.2 billion. 

The Hong Kong Monetary Authority Exchange Fund was established in 1993 and consists of the Reserve Fund, designed to support the Hong Kong dollar to US dollar exchange rate, and the Future Generations Investment Fund.

77% of the assets are in shares, the rest in government bonds. Almost 90% of these are US dollar-denominated securities.  The main purpose of the fund is to maintain the stability of the financial and monetary systems of Hong Kong and to preserve Hong Kong's status as an international financial center.

  • The fund is divided into 3 portfolios by structure: 
  • Insurance - is a safety cushion for the monetary system of Hong Kong. Consists of highly liquid dollar securities. 
  • Investment - aimed at long-term investments. Consists of shares and bonds of OECD countries. 
  • Strategic – investments in shares of the Hong Kong Stock Exchange and Clearing Center. 
  • It has 2 subsidiaries: RGIC – Real Gate Investment Company – created for investments in two different joint ventures in real estate abroad and EFIC – Eight Finance Investment Company – deals with alternative investments. 

Sovereign fund of the Kingdom of Saudi Arabia Public Investment Fund — $380 billion

Public Investment Fund (PIF) is the investment arm of the government of Saudi Arabia. The fund was founded in 1971 in Riyadh. The fund is headed by the 65-year-old Secretary General Mansour bin Saleh al Maiman. 

The fund provides financial support for commercial projects that are strategically important for the development of the Saudi Arabian economy, but cannot be implemented by private investors alone due to insufficient experience or capital resources. 

The fund mainly invests in the oil and gas sector - oil refineries and further processing, pipelines and storage systems for raw materials and semi-finished products, transportation, energy, water desalination and infrastructure. 

The fund has large stakes in 26 national companies, including the largest bank in the kingdom. At the same time, PIF is actively working as a creditor.

As of the end of 2011, the volume of issued loans exceeded $32 billion. At the same time, the fund lends to companies in Saudi Arabia, practically without going beyond its borders.

Asian Development Bank

(English Asian Development Bank) is a bank founded in 1966, its main goal is to stimulate economic growth in Asia and the Far East by directing loans and providing technical assistance to these regions. 

Headquartered in Manila (Philippines). Both governments of participating countries and legal entities can apply for loans from the ADB. The average amount of one loan is $15 million. 

On August 20, 2015, the Asian Development Bank approved a $1 billion loan to Kazakhstan, which had previously announced the devaluation of the tenge, which should smooth out the economic consequences of establishing a free exchange rate for the national currency[7]. 

Priority investment areas are transport and communications, industry, energy, agriculture, and the financial sector. 

Loans are issued from two funds: regular and special. The first is intended for lending on commercial terms, that is, at a market interest rate and for a shorter period, usually 15-25 years. The special fund is intended for long-term lending - for 25-40 years, at a preferential interest rate (1-3%). The grace period for loans from the regular fund is 3-5 years, and from the special fund, as a rule, 10 years. In addition to loans, ADB's other instruments for helping its member countries include technical assistance, guarantees, and equity purchases. 

It plans to allocate financing that will be used to support projects operating in five priority sectors: renewable energy, industry, advanced technology, health, and food security.
